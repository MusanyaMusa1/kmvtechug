What is Evidence?

Video~12 minEvidence Act Cap. 8

NotebookLM audio discussion. PDF notes downloadable below.

Video Summary

This video introduces the concept of evidence using a real Kampala murder scenario. It explains the two categories under Section 2 of the Evidence Act Cap. 8 oral evidence and documentary evidence including electronic records and the three core reasons courts require evidence: establishing facts, protecting against wrongful decisions, and ensuring fairness.

Opening Scenario

Scenario

A man is found dead in his house in Kampala. His neighbour says she heard shouting the night before. A kitchen knife with blood is found near the body. The accused's fingerprints are on the knife. Two witnesses saw the accused leaving at midnight. A threatening letter written by the accused was found in the dead man's pocket.

Each of these things is evidence. Before a court decides guilt or innocence, it examines all of this carefully: what to accept, what to reject, and how much weight to give each piece. This is what evidence law governs.

Evidence in Ordinary Language

In everyday life, evidence means anything that helps establish whether something is true or false. A student's attendance register entry is evidence he was present. A trader's receipt is evidence she paid. A CCTV recording is evidence the traffic light was green.

Evidence in Law

In law, evidence has a more precise meaning. Not everything that might help establish truth is automatically accepted by a court. The law sets rules about what can be presented, how, and what the court can do with it.

Evidence Act Cap. 8 - Section 2

Evidence includes all statements the court permits or requires from witnesses in relation to matters of fact under inquiry, and all documents including electronic records produced for inspection of the court.

Two broad categories: (1) Oral evidence - witness testimony under oath; (2) Documentary evidence - written or recorded material including emails, texts, and digital files.

Why Courts Need Evidence

Courts cannot guess, assume, or act on personal knowledge of the judge. Every finding must be based on material properly placed before the court. Three reasons evidence is required:

To establish facts. Most disputes involve disagreement about what happened. Evidence is the tool courts use to find out.

To protect against wrongful decisions. Without proper proof requirements, courts could convict innocent people. Evidence law provides safeguards.

To ensure fairness. Both sides must have the opportunity to present their case and challenge the other side's evidence.

Examples in Ugandan Courts

Type of CaseTypes of Evidence Used
Murder trial - High Court UgandaMurder weapon, pathologist evidence, eyewitness testimony, confession to magistrate

Facts vs Evidence

A fact is something that either happened or did not. A evidence is the material used to prove or disprove a fact in court. The court examines evidence to decide what facts have been proved. It works only with what parties bring before it through the rules of evidence.

Key Authority

Authority

Evidence Act Cap. 8, Laws of Uganda - primary statute governing all evidence law in Uganda.

Spear Motors Ltd v Attorney General HCCS No. 692 of 2007 - High Court emphasised the importance and weight of properly produced documentary evidence in civil proceedings.

Key Points - Page 1.1.1
  • Evidence is any material (oral, documentary, or electronic) courts use to determine disputed facts
  • Evidence Act Cap. 8 is the governing statute - know it by its cap number
  • Courts cannot guess - every finding must be based on properly presented material
  • Three purposes: establishing facts, protecting against wrongful decisions, ensuring fairness
  • A fact is what happened; evidence is the material used to prove it
