What is Evidence?

Section 1 Opening Scenario
📍 Scenario — Read This First

A man is found dead in his house in Kampala. His neighbour says she heard shouting from the house the night before. A kitchen knife with blood on it is found near the body. The accused's fingerprints are on the knife. The accused was seen leaving the house at midnight by two witnesses. A letter written by the accused was found in the dead man's pocket threatening him.

Each of these things — the neighbour's account of what she heard, the knife, the fingerprints, the witnesses who saw the accused leaving, and the threatening letter — is evidence.

Before a court can decide whether the accused is guilty or innocent, it must examine all of this carefully. It must decide what to accept, what to reject, and how much weight to give to each piece. This is what evidence law governs.

Section 2 Meaning of Evidence in Ordinary Language

In everyday life, when we say we have evidence of something, we mean we have information, facts, or material that supports or proves a claim.

If a student says he was present in class, his attendance register entry is evidence. If a trader says she paid for goods, her receipt is evidence. If a driver says the traffic light was green, a working CCTV recording of the junction at that moment is evidence.

Evidence in ordinary language means anything that helps establish whether something is true or false.

Section 3 Meaning of Evidence in Law

In law, evidence has a more precise and controlled meaning. Not everything that might help establish the truth is automatically accepted by a court. The law sets rules about what can be presented, how it must be presented, and what the court can do with it.

The Evidence Act Cap. 8 of the Laws of Uganda is the primary statute governing evidence in Uganda. It sets out the framework within which courts decide what information they will receive and consider.

📜 Key Authority — Evidence Act Cap. 8

Section 2 of the Evidence Act Cap. 8 defines evidence to include all statements which the court permits or requires to be made before it by witnesses in relation to matters of fact under inquiry. It also includes all documents including electronic records produced for the inspection of the court.

This means evidence in law covers two broad categories:

First, oral evidence — what witnesses say in court when they testify under oath or affirmation.

Second, documentary evidence — written or recorded material produced before the court, including electronic records such as emails, text messages, and digital files.

Section 4 Why Courts Need Evidence

Courts cannot guess. Courts cannot assume. Courts cannot act on rumour, suspicion, or personal knowledge of the judge.

Every finding a court makes must be based on material that has been properly placed before it. This is a fundamental principle of the justice system.

There are three core reasons why courts require evidence:

The first reason is to establish facts. Most legal disputes involve a disagreement about what happened. Evidence is the tool the court uses to find out what actually occurred.

The second reason is to protect against wrongful decisions. Without rules requiring proper proof, courts could convict innocent people or hold parties liable for things they did not do. Evidence law provides the safeguards that protect against this.

The third reason is to ensure fairness. Both sides in a dispute — whether civil or criminal — must have the opportunity to present their case and challenge the other side's case. Evidence law creates the framework for this fair contest.

Section 5 Examples of Evidence in Ugandan Courts

Ugandan courts deal with evidence every day across a wide range of cases. Here are practical examples drawn from Ugandan legal practice:

Type of Case Types of Evidence Used
Murder trial — High Court of Uganda Murder weapon, medical evidence from a pathologist, eyewitness testimony, confession made to a magistrate, alibi witness
Land dispute — Magistrate's Court, Kampala Certificate of title, sale agreement, witness testimony about long possession, photographs of developments
Civil contract dispute — trader suing for unpaid goods Delivery notes, receipts, correspondence between parties, bank statement showing no payment received
Family law matter Marriage certificate, photographs, testimony of family members, financial records
Section 6 The Relationship Between Facts and Evidence

It is important to understand the difference between a fact and evidence.

A fact is something that either happened or did not happen. It exists in the real world. The accused either stabbed the victim or he did not. The contract was either signed or it was not.

Evidence is the material used to prove or disprove a fact in court. The knife is evidence. The witness who saw the stabbing is evidence. The signed document is evidence.

The court's job is to examine the evidence and decide what facts have been proved. The court does not go into the world and investigate for itself. It works only with what the parties bring before it through the rules of evidence.

Section 7 Key Authorities
📚 Primary Authority

Evidence Act Cap. 8, Laws of Uganda — This is the primary statute. Every student of evidence law in Uganda must know this Act well. It governs what is admissible, how facts are proved, what witnesses can say, and how documents are treated.

Spear Motors Ltd v Attorney General HCCS No. 692 of 2007 — The High Court of Uganda emphasised the importance of documentary evidence in civil proceedings and the weight courts attach to properly produced documents.

Section 8 Summary of Key Points
Key Points — Page 1.1.1
  • Evidence is any material — oral, documentary, or electronic — that a court uses to determine facts in dispute
  • The Evidence Act Cap. 8 is the governing statute for evidence law in Uganda
  • Courts require evidence because they cannot assume, guess, or act on personal knowledge
  • Evidence serves three purposes: establishing facts, protecting against wrongful decisions, and ensuring fairness
  • There is a clear distinction between a fact (what happened) and evidence (what is used to prove it)
  • Ugandan courts apply these rules daily across criminal, civil, land, family, and commercial disputes
💬 Discussion Point

Can a judge decide a case without any evidence at all? What would happen to the justice system if courts were allowed to rely on what the judge personally knows or believes? Use examples from Ugandan courts to illustrate your answer.
