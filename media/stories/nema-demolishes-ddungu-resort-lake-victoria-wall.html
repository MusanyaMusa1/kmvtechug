NEMA Cracks Down on Lake Victoria Encroachment: Demolition of Illegal Perimeter Wall at Ddungu Resort Signals Renewed Enforcement

KM
King Musah Media News Desk
Published 21 July 2026 · English
📷 An excavator working near a lakeshore

The National Environment Management Authority is demonstrating resolve in safeguarding Uganda's critical ecosystems, continuing the demolition of an illegal perimeter wall constructed inside Lake Victoria by Ddungu Resort Beach in Mulungu, Makindye Division. Operations that began on July 17, 2026, resumed this week, underscoring the agency's mandate to protect the lake from unregulated private developments.

Footage released by NEMA shows heavy machinery, including an excavator on a pontoon, systematically dismantling the wall and removing debris from the water. The authority framed the action as a firm reaffirmation of its commitment to sustainable development amid growing pressures on Lake Victoria, Africa's largest freshwater body, which supports millions of livelihoods through fishing, transport, water supply, and tourism.

NEMA's move sends a clear message that even commercial ventures in sensitive zones must comply with setbacks, environmental impact assessments, and permitting requirements. Critics point to perceived inconsistencies in high-profile cases elsewhere, arguing for more proactive monitoring and stronger penalties to prevent encroachments rather than relying on costly remedial demolitions, while supporters note resource constraints in policing a vast lake against powerful interests.

Uganda's environmental laws empower NEMA to regulate developments near water bodies and enforce restoration, aligning with regional efforts under frameworks like the Lake Victoria Basin Commission. NEMA's visible enforcement at Ddungu Resort is a welcome step in protecting a shared natural heritage; as pressures on Lake Victoria intensify, consistent application of the law will determine whether Uganda reverses degradation or risks irreversible loss.
