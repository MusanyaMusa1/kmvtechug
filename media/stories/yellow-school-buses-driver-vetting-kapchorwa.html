Government Announces Uniform Yellow School Vehicles and Strict Driver Vetting After Kapchorwa Tragedy Claims 24 Lives

KM
King Musah Media News Desk
Published 20 July 2026 · English
📷 A yellow school bus parked on a roadside

In a decisive response to the deadly Kapchorwa bus crash that claimed the lives of 24 pupils from King David Junior School Ndejje, the Government of Uganda has unveiled new safety regulations for school transportation, including mandatory uniform colouring of vehicles and rigorous vetting of drivers.

Minister of State for Transport, Julius Maganda Wandera, announced that all vehicles used for mass transportation of learners will adopt a uniform colour — widely expected to be yellow — to improve visibility and road safety. Additionally, all drivers will be required to obtain special government-issued badges and security clearance from the Ministry of Works and Transport before they can operate school transport.

The announcement comes as the death toll from the accident rose to 24, with three more pupils succumbing to injuries sustained when their bus overturned at Chekwatit village in Kawowo Sub-county, Kapchorwa District. The pupils were returning from an educational tour to Sipi Falls. The school's founder and director, Mr. Tadeo Ssekade, was among those who perished.

Broader crackdown on school trips. The new measures form part of a wider emergency response. The Ministry of Education and Sports has already suspended all educational trips and study tours nationwide. Schools currently on excursions have been ordered to report to the nearest police station for guidance and escorted return. Existing guidelines — including pre-trip approvals, roadworthy vehicle checks, daylight travel only, and police notification — will now be reviewed and strengthened.

Public reaction: praise, scepticism, tough questions. Many Ugandans welcome the move as long overdue, arguing it aligns Uganda with international best practices where dedicated school buses are easily identifiable, professionally maintained, and operated by vetted drivers. Critics, however, highlight enforcement challenges: most schools rely on rented buses for one-off trips rather than maintaining permanent, well-maintained fleets, and question how a fresh coat of paint and badges will address mechanical failures, driver fatigue, or poor road conditions if enforcement remains weak.

"Why introduce uniform colours when the real issue is rented, poorly maintained vehicles and lack of consistent inspection?" one analyst posted, reflecting widespread sentiment. Standardising vehicle colour and driver credentials is a positive, practical step toward accountability and visibility, but without robust enforcement — mandatory pre-trip mechanical inspections by certified garages, real-time GPS tracking, and severe penalties for non-compliant operators — the rules risk becoming another well-intentioned policy gathering dust.

The pain of the families who lost 24 innocent children — bright young learners whose only "crime" was going on an educational tour — cannot be overstated. Their deaths must catalyse genuine reform, not just announcements. King Musah Media will continue monitoring the implementation of these new rules and the full investigation into the Kapchorwa crash.
