Repatriation and Reintegration: Ugandan Returnees from South Africa Embrace Home and Opportunity at Kyankwanzi

KM
King Musah Media News Desk
Published 19 July 2026 · English
📷 Returnees gathered for an orientation programme at a national institute

In a demonstration of decisive leadership and national solidarity, the Government of Uganda, under President Yoweri Kaguta Museveni, successfully coordinated the voluntary repatriation of over 1,100 Ugandan nationals from South Africa amid escalating xenophobic violence and anti-migrant protests. The operation, which concluded its first phase in early July 2026, has ensured the safety of citizens and paved the way for their psychosocial rehabilitation and reintegration into productive national life.

The repatriation was triggered by rising insecurity in South Africa, where vigilante groups issued ultimatums and protests targeted foreign nationals, resulting in loss of life — including at least one Ugandan — and widespread fear among diaspora communities. President Museveni promptly directed relevant ministries and agencies, including the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, Uganda Airlines, the Uganda High Commission in Pretoria, and security organs, to facilitate the safe return of those who registered for assistance.

A structured reintegration at NALI Kyankwanzi. Upon arrival at Entebbe International Airport in phased charter flights, returnees were transported to the National Leadership Institute (NALI) in Kyankwanzi for orientation, trauma counselling, and civic education. Government officials, including Acting Foreign Affairs Minister Haruna Kyeyune Kasolo and Prime Minister Robinah Nabbanja, clarified that the initiative addresses trauma from violence abroad, provides documentation (including national IDs for children born in South Africa), financial literacy, and guidance on programmes like the Parish Development Model (PDM), Emyooga, and skilling initiatives. Prof. Peter Baguma of Makerere University led sessions on mental health and hope.

Social media reactions have been mixed but predominantly reflective of national pride and optimism, with many Ugandans praising the government's proactive response and the Chief of Defence Forces, Gen. Muhoozi Kainerugaba, for his role in coordinating logistics. Critics on platforms like X questioned the necessity of the centralised programme at Kyankwanzi, with some raising concerns about its framing.

Presidential engagement. President Museveni met with the returnees, delivering what has been described as a message of opportunity, underscoring Uganda's progress in agro-industrialisation, exports, and middle-income aspirations. Returnees were encouraged to leverage their skills, global exposure, and resilience gained abroad to contribute to national development.

Challenges remain — economic opportunities must match the rhetoric, and compensation for losses abroad warrants transparent handling. As one participant noted in UBC footage, experiences abroad have "opened our eyes to the enormous opportunities available at home."
