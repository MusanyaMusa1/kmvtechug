Balaam and Nameere's Anti-Corruption Crusade in Eastern Uganda: Bold Accountability or Political Theatre?

MM
Musanya Musa
Published 19 July 2026 · English
📷 Government officials inspecting a construction site

In the volatile arena of Ugandan local governance, few initiatives have sparked as much polarized debate as the ongoing accountability tours led by Minister for Local Government Balaam Barugahara Ateenyi and State Minister Justine Nameere. Their "Expose the Corrupt in Local Government" campaign has zeroed in on districts like Mbale, Bulambuli, and Jinja, with unannounced visits to schools, health centres, and road projects. Social media, particularly X, has become a battleground — supporters hail them as fearless reformers, while critics, including voices like Nambooze in circulating videos, accuse them of grandstanding, selective targeting, and potentially exacerbating the very problems they claim to solve.

On-the-ground actions: exposing systemic rot? Since assuming office, Balaam and Nameere have conducted high-profile inspections across eastern Uganda. In Mbale, they uncovered alleged ghost workers at Naiku Health Centre III — government payroll listed 19 staff, yet only three were present during a morning visit, with nine identified as fictitious. At Naiku Primary School, a headcount revealed inflated enrolment figures (reported 1,352 pupils versus actual 705), prompting the arrest of the head teacher on suspicion of capitation grant fraud.

Road projects have drawn equal scrutiny. In Bulambuli, the ministers inspected sites like the Tunyi-Kimuli-Bumasoba Road and a Shs500 million bridge, leading to the arrest of District Engineer Paulo Walimbwa over alleged substandard or non-existent works. These actions align with President Museveni's explicit mandate; in June 2026 he defended appointing the pair — describing them as "cantankerous" and "quarrelsome" — precisely because of their willingness to confront entrenched corruption in local governments.

The backlash: optics over due process? Criticism has been swift and substantive. The Uganda Institution of Professional Engineers (UIPE) condemned "arbitrary arrests" and public humiliation of engineers in Mbale and Bulambuli, calling the operations "theatrical grandstanding." UIPE President Eng. Peterson Mwesiga argued that while supporting the anti-corruption agenda, such spectacles undermine professional morale, weaken legal cases, and turn accountability into a "charade." Rubanda District Chairman Steven Ampeire Kasyaba echoed this, labelling the actions as optics-driven, warning that public suspensions before full investigations violate presumption of innocence.

Broader context. Uganda's corruption challenge is well-documented — billions lost annually to leakages in revenue collection, ghost payrolls, and ghost projects, particularly affecting pro-poor initiatives like PDM and UPE. Balaam and Nameere's approach reflects Museveni's frustration with failed monitoring structures; their confrontational style has shaken complacency, yielding immediate headlines and some recoveries. Yet sustainable impact requires follow-through: forensic audits, prosecutions, asset recovery, and systemic reforms rather than episodic tours.

For Ugandans, the litmus test will be results: fewer ghost workers, completed roads of quality, functional schools and hospitals, and recovered funds. As the ministers expand their tours nationwide, balancing zeal with legality will determine whether this becomes a lasting reform or a fleeting spectacle. King Musah Media encourages evidence-based discourse; this analysis draws from official statements, media reports, and public reactions as of July 2026.
