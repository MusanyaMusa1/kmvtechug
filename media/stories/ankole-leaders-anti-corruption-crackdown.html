Ankole Leaders Squirm as Ministers Balaam and Nameere Bring Anti-Corruption Fire to the Region

MM
Musanya Musa
Published 16 July 2026 · English
📷 Government officials addressing a crowd outdoors

Local leaders in Uganda's Ankole region found themselves under the spotlight — and seemingly on the defensive — as Ministers Balaam Barugahara and Justine Nameere pressed ahead with their sweeping crackdown on graft in local governments. The pair, appointed earlier this year with a mandate to shake up underperforming and allegedly corrupt local structures, have intensified nationwide inspections of government projects, exposing shoddy work, misappropriated funds, and systemic failures long complained about by residents.

Footage circulating on social media shows a group of Ankole officials gathered outdoors, facing microphones and cameras, with one prominent figure gesturing emphatically while others stand by with forced smiles. The clip, captioned "Come Slowly, Ankole Region Leaders," has drawn sharp commentary online, with one widely shared post labelling the gathering "faces of corruption fighting the fight against corruption."

The ministers' campaign extends beyond the Parish Development Model to road projects, health worker recruitment, infrastructure maintenance, and general service delivery across districts. In recent weeks their teams have triggered arrests, including a district engineer in Bulambuli over a poorly maintained road that received substantial funding, and have called for the removal of village chairpersons accused of mismanaging funds and colluding in land grabs.

Local reactions range from cautious support to visible discomfort. Some officials are reportedly scrambling to avoid or delay visits, while the Uganda Local Government Association has urged dialogue before further arrests — a pushback that critics say only highlights the depth of the problem the ministers were brought in to solve. President Museveni reportedly chose the pair for their "cantankerous" style, a deliberate move to disrupt the status quo in local governments long criticised as hotspots for waste and patronage.

As the tour continues, many Ugandans are watching closely to see whether the high-profile drive will deliver tangible improvements in service delivery, or whether entrenched interests will find ways to weather the storm.
