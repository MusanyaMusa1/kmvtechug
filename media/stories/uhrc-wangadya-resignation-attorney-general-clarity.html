UHRC Chairperson Mariam Wangadya's Resignation Notice Sparks Legal Clarity from Attorney General Amid Turbulent Tenure

KM
King Musah Media News Desk
Published 21 July 2026 · English
📷 The Uganda Parliament building

Uganda Human Rights Commission Chairperson Justice Mariam Fauzat Wangadya remains in office, the Attorney General has clarified, despite a resignation letter she submitted to President Yoweri Museveni on July 6, 2026. Attorney General Sam Mayanja told Parliament that a mere notice of intention does not constitute a formal resignation; it takes effect only after a formal letter is submitted and accepted by the appointing authority.

Wangadya herself confirmed the resignation intent in media interviews, stating she "could no longer hold on" after years of service. Her brief letter thanked the President for the opportunity to serve but provided no detailed reasons. The development comes shortly after she publicly criticised presidential appointments to the commission, describing it as a "dumping ground for failed politicians."

A tenure marked by challenges. Wangadya's leadership since her 2021 appointment has navigated persistent headwinds, including accusations of internal divisions, with some commissioners petitioning the President and Inspector General of Government over alleged maladministration. An Auditor General report highlighted case backlogs, with many complaints unresolved for over a decade, attributed partly to funding shortages and overload.

Critics argue the institution underperformed on key violations and became entangled in politics; supporters credit her with pushing reforms and exposing issues like parliamentary corruption. The Attorney General's intervention ensures legal continuity in the short term, affirming the UHRC remains fully constituted with staggered member terms, though the episode exposes vulnerabilities in resignation protocols and succession for key constitutional offices. King Musah Media will follow the legal and operational implications.
