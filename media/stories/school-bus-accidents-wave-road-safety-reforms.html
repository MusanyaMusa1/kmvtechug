Tragic Wave of School Bus Accidents Shakes Uganda: Calls Grow for Urgent Road Safety Reforms

KM
King Musah Media News Desk
Published 18 July 2026 · English
📷 A school bus parked on a rural Ugandan road

In just over a week, Uganda has witnessed a series of devastating school bus crashes that have claimed dozens of young lives and left communities reeling, prompting the government to suspend all school trips nationwide and raising serious questions about student transportation safety during educational tours.

The deadliest incident occurred on the evening of Thursday, July 16 or 17, 2026, in Kapchorwa District, eastern Uganda. A bus (registration UA 108BQ) carrying pupils from King David Junior School in Ndejje, Kampala, overturned after reportedly losing control on a steep section of road at Chekwatit Village, Kimawa Parish. The pupils were returning from an educational tour to the popular Sipi Falls.

Official figures vary slightly by source, but reports confirm at least 19–21 deaths, predominantly primary school pupils, along with the school's founder and director, Mr. Tadeo Ssekade. Dozens more, including students and staff, were injured and rushed to local hospitals such as Kapchorwa General Hospital and Mbale Regional Referral Hospital. Preliminary investigations point to possible mechanical failure, with some eyewitness accounts suggesting the vehicle had issues earlier in the journey on the hazardous Chekwatit Hill, a known blackspot for accidents.

Not an isolated incident. This tragedy followed closely on the heels of two other school-related crashes earlier in July. On July 10, a bus hired by Mwebaze High School in Wakiso District collided with a Uganda Railways passenger train at a railway crossing in Namumira-Bukasa Village, Mukono District. One adult female (the wife of the school's head teacher) died at the scene, while several students sustained injuries; police cited speeding and failure to heed signals at the crossing, and the bus driver allegedly fled.

Then on July 14, an Isuzu bus owned by Amuria High School, transporting 73 students from St. Paul Secondary School in Katakwi for a geography tour, rammed into a stationary trailer on the Mbale–Iganga Highway in Bugweri District. The 68-year-old driver, Kasakya Abdallah, died instantly, and 13 students plus others were injured.

Systemic concerns. These incidents have highlighted overloaded or poorly maintained vehicles, long-distance overnight or evening travel for school tours, dangerous road sections, driver fatigue or error, and inadequate enforcement of safety regulations at crossings and on hilly terrain. Uganda already records thousands of road deaths annually, but the involvement of schoolchildren has struck a particularly emotional chord.

Government response. Education Minister John Chrysostom Muyingo announced a temporary ban on all school trips pending full investigations. Authorities are also directing schools to paint transport vehicles yellow for better visibility and considering the closure of King David Junior School. Parliament's Committee on Physical Infrastructure has summoned the Works and Transport Minister for explanations.

Parents, educators, and opposition voices have expressed outrage, with some on social media questioning whether private probes might uncover negligence, corruption in vehicle procurement, or overlooked warnings. Local residents in Kapchorwa reportedly acted as first responders, underscoring gaps in emergency services.

As funerals begin and families mourn, the incidents serve as a stark reminder of the risks children face in pursuit of experiential learning. Experts urge stricter vehicle inspections, professional driver training with limits on hours, better infrastructure at hazard points, and guidelines limiting tour timings and group sizes.

King Musah Media will continue monitoring developments, including police updates on investigations and any charges. For now, the priority remains supporting the bereaved and ensuring no more young lives are lost on Uganda's roads.
